One of the main reasons I wanted to reach out was with regards to a specific assignment I was planning for the class, a “Object-Oriented Exercise”, per say.

  • The purpose of the assignment is to provide an intuitive introduction to object oriented design (i.e. before we have taught any basic object oriented principles)
  • The exercise would take place over an entire class period
  • The exercise would include no actual code written
  • We would separate the class of about 25 into groups of ~4-5 students
    • Each group would be assigned a topic
      • The group would be expected to take the topic and build a modified object-oriented model about that topic
        • We don’t expect students to draw models using standard symbols and notation, but rather for them just to do what feels natural
      • The group would utilize whiteboards to draw their model
      • After approximately 30 minutes, the students would visit each of the other groups and observe their models
        • This provides time for feedback from other students as well as requires each group to defend their models

 

Excellent clear framework.  You know I am partial to group work whenever feasible—but I have found that groups of 3-4 are ideal, and occasionally partners.  For 100 students, it makes sense to have slightly group in the introductory activity.

  1. You mention trying to mine an intuitive introduction formed by the new students.   I would be inclined to want to provide a small example, for fear that groups may not have sufficient experience to showcase inter-related elements of a model.    You might be able to rely on some already published ones from other fields—Generations Tree,  Employee Structure,  Biology Classifications,  Periodic Table Elements—to discuss some “objects” in the model, some “relationships” that are visible in the model, and some “classifications” that are shared by some of the objects.
  2. Your assignment might need to specifically prompt them to identify a minimum number of “distinct” entities, and might also prompt them to identify a minimum number of “categories” for some of those entities.  Finally, you might require a certain number of designated “relationships/connections” be included…  While trying to give that kind of structure always seems awkward, it will guide groups to trying to describe them in their models.   You might even want to mention the wording “IS – A”  and “HAS-A” to be a way to prime the pump.
  3. Whiteboards and multiple colored markers are ideal.  (Sticky notes often are excellent for placing some additional detail into the models;  or can be used to record questions raised by “visitors” during the explore and share times.)
